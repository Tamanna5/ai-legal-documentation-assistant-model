The Constitution of India is the supreme law of the country that lays down the framework for the governance of India. It defines the fundamental political principles, establishes the structure, procedures, powers, and duties of the government institutions, and sets out the fundamental rights, directive principles,and duties of citizens.

The Indian Constitution was adopted on January 26, 1950.

Some salient features include federalism, parliamentary form of government, fundamental rights, directive principles of state policy, and an independent judiciary.

Fundamental rights are a set of rights guaranteed to Indian citizens by the Constitution, including the right to equality, right to freedom, right against exploitation, right to freedom of religion, cultural and educational rights, and right to constitutional remedies.

Directive principles of state policy are guidelines for the government to establish a just society by securing social, economic, and political justice, and promoting the welfare of the people. These principles are not enforceable by any court but are fundamental in governance.

The preamble outlines the objectives of the Indian Constitution, such as justice, liberty, equality, fraternity, and ensuring the dignity of the individual and unity and integrity of the nation.

The Supreme Court is the highest judicial authority and has the power of judicial review, interpreting the Constitution, and resolving disputes between the Union and states or between states.

Article 370 provided special autonomous status to the state of Jammu and Kashmir, granting it certain privileges and autonomy. However, it was abrogated in August 2019.

The President is elected by an electoral college consisting of elected members of both houses of Parliament and elected members of the legislative assemblies of states and union territories.

The President can be impeached for violation of the Constitution by a two-thirds majority of members present and voting in both houses of Parliament.

Article 21 guarantees the right to life and personal liberty, which has been interpreted broadly by the judiciary to encompass various other rights, including the right to privacy.

Formation of new states requires a constitutional amendment, which may be initiated by a resolution in Parliament and requires a simple majority.

The Indian Constitution recognizes Hindi and English as official languages of the Union, with additional languages recognized by states.

The Election Commission is responsible for conducting free and fair elections to Parliament and state legislatures, ensuring electoral integrity and fairness.

The Indian Constitution serves as a bulwark against arbitrary state action and protects individual rights through various provisions, including fundamental rights and judicial review. These questions cover a range of topics related to the Indian Constitution and can serve as a basis for discussions between lawyers and their clients.

A national emergency can be declared under Article 352 of the Constitution by the President on the advice of the Council of Ministers. It grants the central government extraordinary powers to deal with threats to the country's security.

Article 370 granted special autonomous status to the state of Jammu and Kashmir, allowing it to have its own constitution and limited autonomy over internal affairs. However, it was abrogated in August 2019, thereby integrating Jammu and Kashmir fully into the Indian Union.

Fundamental rights can be amended through a constitutional amendment passed by both houses of Parliament with a two-thirds majority. However, amendments cannot violate the basic structure of the Constitution.

The Indian Constitution prohibits discrimination on various grounds, including race, religion, caste, sex, or place of birth. It guarantees equality before the law and equal protection of laws to all citizens.

Judges of the Supreme Court are appointed by the President on the advice of the Chief Justice of India and other senior judges. Judges of High Courts are appointed by the President in consultation with the Chief Justice of India, the Governor of the state, and the Chief Justice of the High Court.

The Attorney General of India is the chief legal advisor to the government and assists the government in legal matters. They also represent the government in legal proceedings and perform other duties as prescribed by the Constitution or law.

The right to constitutional remedies (Article 32 for the Supreme Court and Article 226 for High Courts) enables individuals to enforce their fundamental rights by directly approaching the Supreme Court or High Courts for appropriate remedies, such as writs.

Judges of the Supreme Court and High Courts can be removed only through a process of impeachment, which requires a motion passed by a special majority in both houses of Parliament on grounds of proven misbehavior or incapacity.

The Indian Constitution promotes social justice and equality through various provisions, including reservations for disadvantaged groups in education and employment, affirmative action programs, and protective discrimination measures.

The framers of the Constitution took note of the fact that certain communities in the country were suffering from extreme social, educational and economic backwardness on account of the primitive agricultural practices, lack of infrastructure facilities and geographical isolation. The Constitution of India in Article 366 (25) prescribe that the Scheduled Tribes means such tribes or tribal communities as are deemed under Article 342 of the Constitution to be Scheduled Tribes.

There are over 700 tribes (with overlapping communities in more than one State) which have been notified under Article 342 of the Constitution of India, spread over different States and Union Territories of the country. The largest number of main tribal communities (62) has been specified in relation to the State of Orissa. The Scheduled Tribes have been specified in relation to all the States and Union Territories except Haryana, Punjab, Chandigarh, Delhi, and Pondicherry.

The Constitution of India seeks to secure for all its citizens, among other things, social and economic justice, equality of status and opportunity and assures the dignity of the individual. All Rights available to the Citizens of India, enshrined in the Constitution or any law of the land or any order of the Government are equally available to the Scheduled Tribes also.
